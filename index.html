Economic Power Simulator

A teaching tool showing how incentives shape policy choices and economic outcomes.
Inspired by Milton Friedman's approach to institutional analysis.

How This Works

You're the President in early 2026. Each decision creates trade-offs—short-term wins often have long-term costs, and vice versa. The goal isn't to "win" but to see how different choices affect your economy, credibility, and accountability.

Current Situation

Welcome to Q1 2026. The economy is growing at 4.2%, unemployment sits at 3.8% (near full employment), and inflation has cooled to 2.1%. But there's tension building with Iran, and crude oil prices are edging up from $75 toward $80 per barrel.

What do you do?

Cut Interest Rates (0.25%) The Fed has been holding rates steady. Business leaders are pressuring you to push for cuts before the election.

Short-term: Markets rally, +2% approval | Long-term: Risks inflation if oil keeps rising
Increase Government Spending (+$50B Infrastructure) Propose a new infrastructure package funded by deficit spending.

Short-term: Creates jobs, +0.6% GDP | Long-term: Adds inflation pressure
Publicly Pressure Fed to Cut Rates Hold a press conference criticizing the Fed for being too slow. Demand they cut rates now.

Short-term: Markets rally, +3% approval | Long-term: Erodes Fed credibility permanently
Do Nothing / Trust Institutions Let the Federal Reserve make independent decisions. Maintain institutional independence.

Short-term: No immediate win | Long-term: Preserves credibility, but oil may spike naturally
